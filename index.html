Contemplative Prayer

A Journey into Silent Communion with God

Introduction to Contemplative Prayer

+

Contemplative prayer is the practice of resting in God's presenceA state of peaceful awareness where we simply abide with God without agenda or effort, moving beyond words and thoughts into silent attention and deep spiritual awareness.

This ancient Christian practice invites us into a profound intimacy with the Divine, where we learn to be still and know that God is God. It's not about achieving anything, but about surrendering to the love that already surrounds us.

Biblical Foundations

+

"Be still, and know that I am God"

Psalm 46:10

The Hebrew word for "be still" is raphahMeans to let go, release, or cease striving. It suggests a complete surrender of our efforts and anxieties., which means to let go, to cease striving. This isn't passive inactivity, but an active surrender to God's presence.

After the earthquake a fire, but the LORD was not in the fire. And after the fire a gentle whisper. When Elijah heard it, he pulled his cloak over his face and went out and stood at the mouth of the cave.

1 Kings 19:12-13

Elijah discovered God not in the dramatic displays of power, but in the gentle whisperThe Hebrew 'qol demamah daqqah' literally means 'a voice of thin silence' - God's presence in the subtle, quiet moments. This teaches us to listen for God in the silence.

"Remain in me, as I also remain in you."

John 15:4

Jesus calls us to abideThe Greek 'meno' means to stay, dwell, or remain. It implies a continuous, intimate relationship rather than occasional visits in Him - a continuous dwelling that contemplative prayer cultivates.

Early Church & Patristic Practice

+

The Desert Fathers and Mothers of the 3rd-5th centuries developed profound practices of contemplative prayer, seeking God in the silence of the wilderness.

Go, sit in your cell, and your cell will teach you everything.
— Abba Moses

Key Figures & Teachings

Evagrius Ponticus (345-399)

Taught that prayer progresses from praktikeThe active life of virtue and discipline that prepares the soul for deeper prayer (active virtue) to theoriaContemplative vision of God, the highest form of prayer where the mind rests in God beyond concepts (contemplative vision). He emphasized the importance of hesychiaInner stillness and tranquility of heart, free from passionate thoughts and distractions - inner stillness.

John Cassian (360-435)

Brought Desert wisdom to the West, teaching the practice of continuous prayer through short, repeated phrases that anchor the heart in God.

This is the goal of the monk and the perfection of his heart: to persevere continuously in prayer.
— John Cassian

Gregory of Nyssa (335-395)

Described the soul's journey into the divine darknessNot absence of God, but God's overwhelming presence that transcends human understanding - a luminous darkness, where God is encountered beyond concepts and images.

Theological Themes

+

Union with God

Contemplative prayer seeks theosisDeification or becoming partakers of the divine nature (2 Peter 1:4) - not becoming God, but sharing in God's life - participation in the divine life. We don't lose our identity but discover our truest self in God.

Attentiveness & Presence

This prayer cultivates pure attention - a quality of presence that is alert yet relaxed, focused yet open. It's learning to be fully present to the Present One.

Interior Transformation

Through regular practice, contemplative prayer gradually transforms our inner landscape, creating space for God's love to work within us. It's not about changing God's mind, but allowing God to change our hearts.

Prayer is not asking. Prayer is putting oneself in the hands of God, at His disposition, and listening to His voice in the depth of our hearts.
— Mother Teresa

  2. Meditatio: What is God saying to me?

Church Fathers Commentary

+
When the mind has put off the old self and shall put on the one born of grace, then it will see its own state in the time of prayer resembling sapphire or the color of heaven.
— Evagrius Ponticus

Evagrius describes the transformation of consciousness in deep prayer, where the mind becomes like heaven itself - clear, luminous, and peaceful.

We must above all observe this rule: to begin by purifying our thoughts, so that we may be able to offer pure prayers to God.
— John Cassian

Cassian emphasizes that contemplative prayer requires ongoing purification - not perfection, but a sincere desire to align our hearts with God's love.

The person who has experienced the sweetness of God abandons everything that is opposed to God.
— Isaac the Syrian

Isaac speaks of how authentic encounter with God naturally transforms our desires and priorities, drawing us away from what separates us from divine love.

Prayer is the encounter of God's thirst with ours.
— St. Augustine

Augustine beautifully captures the mutual longing between God and the human heart - prayer as the meeting place of divine and human desire.
